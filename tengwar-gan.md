# Tengwar Mode for Gan (Nanchang)

## Romanization Disclaimer

> **WARNING: No standardized romanization exists for Gan Chinese.**
>
> The romanization scheme used in this document is **project-defined**, created specifically for this Tengwar mode. It is based on Pinfa (拼法) conventions with adaptations for Gan phonology, and is **not a community standard** nor endorsed by any government or academic body.
>
> This romanization is intended solely as a bridge between IPA and Tengwar mappings. Readers familiar with Gan phonology should refer to the IPA transcriptions as the authoritative reference.

---

## Why this mode exists

Gan is the fourth Sinitic language in Compatibility Group A to receive a Tengwar mode, following Mandarin, Cantonese, and Hakka. Nanchang dialect (昌都片/Chang-Du) serves as the representative variety, spoken by approximately 2.4 million people in Jiangxi Province.

Gan shares significant phonological features with Hakka, historically grouped together as "Hakka-Gan dialects" due to parallel developments from Middle Chinese. Both preserve final stops but Gan has undergone *-p > -t merger, retaining only -t and -k codas.

This is a phonemic tehtar mode.

## Romanization scheme definition

This project-defined romanization follows Pinfa conventions where possible, with systematic adaptations for Gan-specific phonology.

### Design principles

1. **Aspirated consonants**: Use aspirated letter (p, t, k, c, q) for aspirated; unaspirated letter (b, d, g, z, j) for unaspirated
2. **Palatal series**: j, q, x for /tɕ, tɕʰ, ɕ/
3. **Vowels**: Standard Pinyin-like notation with additions for Gan-specific sounds
4. **Tones**: Superscript numbers 1-7

## Final consonants

Gan preserves nasal codas and two stop codas from Middle Chinese. Critically, **-p has merged into -t**, leaving only -t and -k as stop finals.

### Nasal finals

| Final | IPA | Tengwa |
|-------|-----|--------|
| -n | /n/ | numen |
| -ng | /ŋ/ | noldo |

Note: Some Nanchang subdialects preserve -m; if needed, use malta.

### Stop finals (unreleased)

| Final | IPA | Tengwa | Notes |
|-------|-----|--------|-------|
| -t | /t̚/ | tinco | Includes historical *-p words |
| -k | /k̚/ | calma | |

**The -p > -t merger:** Historical Middle Chinese *-p coda has merged with -t in Nanchang Gan. Words that have -p in Hakka/Cantonese uniformly appear as -t in Gan. Examples:

| Meaning | Cantonese | Hakka | Gan |
|---------|-----------|-------|-----|
| ten | sap | sip | set |
| enter | jap | ngip | nit |
| answer | daap | dap | dat |

This merger is systematic and complete; there are no -p codas in Nanchang Gan.

## Tones

Nanchang has seven tones: five in open syllables (Ping/Shang/Qu categories) and two checked tones (Ru category) on syllables with stop codas.

### Tone inventory

| Tone | Category | Chao | Description | Mark |
|------|----------|------|-------------|------|
| T1 | Yin Ping | 42 | mid-falling | falling stroke |
| T2 | Yang Ping | 24 | low-rising | rising stroke + double-dot |
| T3 | Shang | 213 | dipping | chevron |
| T4 | Yin Qu | 45 | high-rising | rising stroke |
| T5 | Yang Qu | 11 | low-level | flat bar + double-dot |
| T6 | Yin Ru | 5 | high checked | flat bar |
| T7 | Yang Ru | 21 | low checked | falling stroke + double-dot |

### Rationale for tone mark assignments

The extended contour + register system maps shape and pitch height independently:

- **T1 (42):** Mid-falling. Falling stroke alone (starts mid, not high).
- **T2 (24):** Rising from low register. Rising stroke + double-dot (low modifier).
- **T3 (213):** Dipping contour. Chevron mark (same as Mandarin T3).
- **T4 (45):** High-rising. Rising stroke alone (starts high).
- **T5 (11):** Level at low register. Flat bar + double-dot.
- **T6 (5):** High checked (short, high pitch). Flat bar alone; stop coda implies checked.
- **T7 (21):** Low-falling checked. Falling stroke + double-dot; stop coda implies checked.

### Distinguishing T1 (42) from T4 (45)

Both are "rising" in the sense of pitch movement direction from their start points, but:
- T1 starts mid (42) and falls: falling stroke
- T4 starts high (45) and rises further: rising stroke

Alternative analysis: Some phonetic studies describe T1 as [33] (mid-level) and T4 as [35] (mid-rising). If this analysis is adopted:
- T1: flat bar + single-dot (mid-level)
- T4: rising stroke + single-dot (mid-rising)

This specification adopts the 42/45 values from primary sources but notes the variation.

### Tone mark codepoints

| Mark | Composition | Codepoints |
|------|-------------|------------|
| Flat bar | nasalizer-teng | U+E050 |
| Rising stroke | acute-teng | U+E046 |
| Falling stroke | grave-teng | U+E054 |
| Chevron | caron-teng | U+E055 |
| Single dot below | unutixe-teng | U+E045 |
| Double dot below | dotdblbelow-teng | U+E043 |

Tone marks appear below the tengwa bearing the nuclear vowel.

## Compatibility group

Gan belongs to **Group A** (Mandarin/Cantonese/Hakka):
- Grade 1 = voiceless unaspirated
- Grade 2 = voiceless aspirated
- No voiced obstruents

This is compatible with the other Group A languages; a document can mix Gan with Mandarin/Cantonese/Hakka text if properly marked for tone system differences.

## Font requirements

Gan requires no new glyphs beyond those already in Mandarin and Hakka modes:

- **palatal mark**: From Mandarin mode
- **unutixe**: Standard syllabic marker
- **underdot**: For schwa (if used)
- **chevron (caron-teng)**: From Mandarin T3
- **extended tone marks**: From Cantonese/Hakka modes

All glyphs are available in Alcarin Extended.

## Open questions

1. **Subdialect variation:** Some Nanchang subdialects may preserve -m or show different tone values. This spec covers mainstream Nanchang city pronunciation.

2. **Tone value variation:** Phonetic studies report variation in exact contour values (e.g., T1 as 42 vs 33, T4 as 45 vs 35). The semantic system accommodates this by mapping contour *shape* rather than exact pitch numbers.

3. **Romanization standardization:** If a community-standard Gan romanization emerges, this spec should be updated to reference it.
