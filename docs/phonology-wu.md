# Wu Chinese (Shanghainese) Phonology Reference

This document provides a comprehensive phonological inventory of Wu Chinese, focusing on **Shanghainese** (Shanghai dialect, Zanhei) as the primary variety, for use as the foundation for designing a Tengwar mode. Shanghainese is the most widely studied Wu variety and represents the Northern Wu (Taihu) branch.

## Overview: What Makes Wu Distinctive

Wu Chinese is phonologically distinctive among Sinitic languages for several critical reasons:

1. **Preservation of Middle Chinese voiced obstruents**: Wu is the only major Chinese language group that retains the full three-way laryngeal contrast of Middle Chinese initial consonants: voiceless unaspirated, voiceless aspirated, and voiced. Mandarin and Cantonese lost voiced obstruents entirely.

2. **Register-tone system**: The voiced/voiceless contrast conditions a yin/yang tone register split. Voiceless initials only occur with high-register (yin) tones; voiced initials only with low-register (yang) tones.

3. **Left-dominant tone sandhi**: Unlike Mandarin and Southern Min (which are right-dominant), Shanghainese spreads the tone of the first syllable across the entire prosodic word.

4. **Simplified coda inventory**: Historical stop codas /-p, -t, -k/ have merged into a single glottal stop /-ʔ/. The only other coda is a nasal /-n~-ŋ/.

5. **Rich vowel system**: Shanghainese has approximately 10-15 distinct vowel qualities with systematic differences between open and checked syllables.

## 1. Consonant Inventory

### Overview

Shanghainese has approximately **28-30 consonant phonemes**, significantly more than Mandarin (24) or Cantonese (17). This richness derives primarily from the three-way laryngeal contrast in stops and affricates.

### The Three-Way Laryngeal Contrast

The defining feature of Wu phonology is the three-way contrast in obstruents:

| Type | Description | IPA Example | Phonation |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Voiceless unaspirated** | Fortis, no aspiration | /p, t, k/ | Modal voice |
| **Voiceless aspirated** | Fortis, with aspiration | /pʰ, tʰ, kʰ/ | Modal voice |
| **Voiced** | Lenis, phonologically voiced | /b, d, g/ | Breathy voice (word-initial) |

**Critical note on voiced consonants**: In word-initial stressed position, the "voiced" consonants are phonetically realized with slack voice (breathy phonation) rather than true voicing. They become truly voiced in intervocalic position. The breathiness functions as a phonemic depressor, lowering the pitch register.

#### Plosives (9 phonemes)

| IPA | Romanization | Description | Comparison |
|-----|--------------|-------------|------------|
| /p/ | p | voiceless unaspirated bilabial | Like French *peu*, English *spy* |
| /pʰ/ | ph | voiceless aspirated bilabial | Like English *pencil* |
| /b/ | b | voiced bilabial | Like English *boy* |
| /t/ | t | voiceless unaspirated alveolar | Like French *temps*, English *sty* |
| /tʰ/ | th | voiceless aspirated alveolar | Like English *toad* |

#### Affricates (6 phonemes)

Shanghainese has alveolo-palatal affricates (not alveolar as in some Wu varieties):

| IPA | Romanization | Description |
|-----|--------------|-------------|
| /tɕ/ | c | voiceless unaspirated alveolo-palatal |
| /tɕʰ/ | ch | voiceless aspirated alveolo-palatal |
| /dʑ/ | j | voiced alveolo-palatal |

**Note**: Some analyses include alveolar affricates /ts, tsʰ, dz/ as separate phonemes, while others treat them as allophones of the palatals before non-front vowels.

#### Fricatives (8 phonemes)

| IPA | Romanization | Description | Comparison |
|-----|--------------|-------------|------------|
| /f/ | f | voiceless labiodental | Like English *fiber* |
| /v/ | v | voiced labiodental | Like English *viper* (no Mandarin equivalent) |
| /s/ | s | voiceless alveolar | Like English *Suzy* |
| /z/ | z | voiced alveolar | Like English *zoo* (no Mandarin equivalent) |
| /ɕ/ | sh | voiceless alveolo-palatal | Similar to Mandarin *x* |
| /ʑ/ | zh | voiced alveolo-palatal | No Mandarin equivalent |
| /h/ | h | voiceless glottal | Like English *high* (NOT like Mandarin /x/) |
| /ɦ/ | gh | voiced glottal | Like Dutch *g*, breathy quality |

**Critical**: The voiced fricatives /v, z, ʑ, ɦ/ are a key distinguishing feature of Wu. They have no Mandarin equivalent.

#### Nasals (4 phonemes)

| IPA | Romanization | Description |
|-----|--------------|-------------|
| /m/ | m | bilabial nasal |
| /n/ | n | alveolar nasal |
| /ɲ/ | ny | palatal nasal (limited distribution) |
| /ŋ/ | ng | velar nasal (initial and syllabic) |

#### Lateral and Approximants

| IPA | Romanization | Description |
|-----|--------------|-------------|
| /l/ | l | alveolar lateral (may be tap [ɾ] in rapid speech) |

### Syllabic Consonants

Shanghainese has four syllabic sonorants that function as complete syllables:

| IPA | Romanization | Example |
|-----|--------------|---------|
| /m̩/ | m | 唔 "not" |
| /n̩/ | n | (limited) |
| /ŋ̩/ | ng | 五 "five" |
| /l̩/ or /ɚ/ | er/l | 而 "and/but" |

### Glottal Stop

The glottal stop /ʔ/ is not an initial consonant but appears as the only stop coda (see Syllable Structure).

## 2. Vowel Inventory

### Overview

Shanghainese has a rich vowel system with approximately **10-15 vowel qualities**, depending on analysis. A key distinction exists between vowels in open syllables (unchecked) and closed syllables (checked with glottal stop).

### Open Syllable Vowels (Unchecked Tones)

These vowels occur in syllables without a glottal stop coda, carrying tones T1, T2, or T3:

| IPA | Romanization | Description | Example |
|-----|--------------|-------------|---------|
| /i/ | i | close front unrounded | 衣 "clothes" |
| /y/ | ü | close front rounded | 雨 "rain" |
| /e/ | ei | close-mid front unrounded | 北 |
| /ɛ/ | e | open-mid front unrounded | 百 |
| /ø/ | eu | close-mid front rounded | 靴 |
| /ɤ/ | eu | close-mid back unrounded | 河 |
| /ɑ/ | a | open back unrounded | 大 "big" |
| /ɔ/ | o/ao | open-mid back rounded | 好 |
| /o/ | ou | close-mid back rounded | 多 |
| /u/ | u | close back rounded | 烏 |
| /ɯ/ | (rare) | close back unrounded | (limited) |

### Closed Syllable Vowels (Checked Tones)

These shorter vowels occur in syllables closed by a glottal stop /-ʔ/, carrying tones T4 or T5:

| IPA | Romanization | Description |
|-----|--------------|-------------|
| /ɪ/ | iq | near-close near-front unrounded |
| /ʏ/ | üq | near-close near-front rounded |
| /ə/ | eq | mid central |
| /ɐ/ | aq | near-open central |
| /ʊ/ | oq | near-close near-back rounded |

**Key observation**: Vowels in checked syllables are systematically more centralized and shorter (approximately 2.4 times shorter in isolation) than their open-syllable counterparts.

### Nasal Rhymes

Unlike Hokkien, Shanghainese does **not** have phonemically nasalized vowels. However, it has nasal codas:

| Romanization | IPA | Description |
|--------------|-----|-------------|
| -an | /ɑ̃/ or /an/ | |
| -en | /ən/ | |
| -in | /in/ | |
| -on | /ɔn/ | |
| -un | /un/ | |
| -ün | /yn/ | |

**Note**: There is only one nasal final phoneme in Shanghainese (unlike Mandarin's /-n/ vs /-ŋ/ distinction). The nasal is realized as [n] ~ [ŋ] in free variation, with palatal [ɲ] after front vowels and velar [ŋ] after back vowels.

### Medials (Glides)

Three medials can combine with main vowels:

| Medial | IPA | Examples |
|--------|-----|----------|
| i- | /j/ | ia, io, iu |
| u- | /w/ | ua, uo |
| ü- | /ɥ/ | (limited) |

### Diphthongs

Shanghainese is often described as having "no true diphthongs" (unlike Cantonese), with apparent diphthongs analyzed as medial + vowel sequences:

| Romanization | IPA | Analysis |
|--------------|-----|----------|
| ia | /iɑ/ | /j/ + /ɑ/ |
| io | /iɔ/ | /j/ + /ɔ/ |
| ua | /uɑ/ | /w/ + /ɑ/ |
| uo | /uɔ/ | /w/ + /ɔ/ |

## 3. Tone System

### Citation Tones

Modern Shanghainese has **five phonetically distinct citation tones**. However, due to the yin-yang register split conditioned by onset voicing, some analyses reduce this to a **two-way phonemic contrast** (falling vs. rising) with register allophony.

| Tone | Name | Chao Value | Contour | Onset Type | Syllable Type |
|------|------|------------|---------|------------|---------------|
| T1 | 陰平 (yin level) | 53 (or 52) | high falling | voiceless | open |
| T2 | 陰上 (yin rising) | 34 (or 35) | high rising | voiceless | open |
| T3 | 陽去 (yang departing) | 13 (or 23) | low rising | voiced | open |
| T4 | 陰入 (yin entering) | 55 (or 5) | high short | voiceless | checked (ʔ) |
| T5 | 陽入 (yang entering) | 12 (or 23) | low short | voiced | checked (ʔ) |

**Notes on tone values**: Different scholars transcribe tone values differently. Chen & Gussenhoven (2015) use 53, 34, 23, 55, 12. Xu & Tang (1988) use 53/52, 334/434, 113/223, 55/54, 12/23. The variation reflects both measurement differences and ongoing sound change.

### Register Split (Yin/Yang)

The **yin-yang distinction** is fundamental to Wu tonology:

| Register | Onset Type | Tones | Pitch Range |
|----------|------------|-------|-------------|
| **Yin** (陰) | Voiceless (/p, pʰ, t, tʰ, k, kʰ, ts, tsʰ, s, h/, etc.) | T1, T2, T4 | Higher (f0 starts ~200+ Hz) |
| **Yang** (陽) | Voiced (/b, d, g, dz, z, v, m, n, l, ɦ/, etc.) | T3, T5 | Lower (f0 starts ~120-150 Hz) |

This is **allophonic**: you cannot have a voiced initial with a yin tone, or a voiceless initial with a yang tone. The conditioning factors from Middle Chinese still operate synchronically.

### Checked Tones

Checked tones (T4, T5) occur only on syllables closed by a glottal stop /-ʔ/. These syllables:
- Are phonetically short
- Have more centralized vowels
- Derive from Middle Chinese syllables ending in /-p, -t, -k/

**Historical merger**: All three Middle Chinese stop codas have merged to /-ʔ/ in Shanghainese:
- 濕 "wet" (MC *-p) → /səʔ⁵⁵/
- 失 "lose" (MC *-t) → /səʔ⁵⁵/
- 塞 "block" (MC *-k) → /səʔ⁵⁵/

### Phonemic Analysis

With the yin-yang split being predictable from onset voicing, and checked tones being predictable from syllable structure, one analysis reduces Shanghainese to just **two phonemic tone contrasts**:

| Phonemic Tone | Yin Realization | Yang Realization |
|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Falling/Level | T1 (53) | (merged with rising) |
| Rising | T2 (34) | T3 (13) |
| Checked | T4 (55) | T5 (12) |

## 4. Tone Sandhi

### Left-Dominant Spreading

Shanghainese tone sandhi is fundamentally different from Mandarin or Hokkien. It is **left-dominant**: the tone of the first syllable spreads rightward across the entire prosodic word, while subsequent syllables lose their lexical tones.

**Example** (disyllabic compound):
- 東西 "things" (dōng + xī in Mandarin)
- First syllable: T1 (53)
- Surface realization: [55-31] — the falling contour spreads across both syllables

### Sandhi Patterns by Initial Tone

| First Syllable Tone | Surface Pattern (disyllabic) | Surface Pattern (trisyllabic) |
|---------------------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| T1 (53) | 55-31 | 55-33-31 |
| T2 (34) | 33-44 | 33-44-31 |
| T3 (13) | 11-23 | 11-22-23 |
| T4 (55) | 55-21 | 55-33-21 |
| T5 (12) | 11-23 | 11-22-23 |

### Two Sandhi Types

Shanghainese has two distinct sandhi patterns:

1. **Rightward tone extension** (for compounds/phonological words):
   - Initial tone spreads across all syllables
   - Used for modifier-noun, compound nouns, etc.
   - Example: 冰棒 "popsicle" (T3 initial) → [22-44]

2. **Tonal reduction** (for phrases/non-lexicalized sequences):
   - Final syllable keeps citation tone
   - Non-final syllables reduce to level pitch
   - Used for verb-noun phrases, coordinate structures

### Sandhi Domain

Sandhi applies within **prosodic words**, which roughly correspond to:
- Compound words
- Modifier + head noun
- Verb + object (lexicalized)
- Number + classifier + noun

The rightmost syllable in a phrase (but within a prosodic word for tone extension) determines whether extension or reduction applies.

## 5. Syllable Structure

### Template

Shanghainese syllable structure:

```
(C) (G) V (C)
```

Where:
- **C** (initial): Any of the ~28 consonants, or null (with possible glottal onset [ʔ])
- **G** (medial/glide): /j/, /w/, or /ɥ/
- **V** (nucleus): Any vowel, or syllabic nasal
- **C** (coda): Only /-n/ (nasal) or /-ʔ/ (glottal stop)

### Coda Inventory

Shanghainese has a **drastically reduced coda inventory** compared to Cantonese or Min:

| Coda | IPA | Notes |
|------|-----|-------|
| Nasal | /-n/ ~ /-ŋ/ | Single nasal phoneme, realized as [n] or [ŋ] depending on vowel |
| Glottal stop | /-ʔ/ | Creates checked syllables (T4, T5) |
| Zero | ∅ | Open syllables |

**Contrast with Cantonese**: Cantonese preserves /-m, -n, -ŋ/ (3 nasal codas) and /-p, -t, -k/ (3 stop codas). Shanghainese has merged these to /-n/ and /-ʔ/ respectively.

### Syllabic Consonants

The syllabic nasals /m̩, n̩, ŋ̩/ and syllabic /l̩~ɚ/ function as complete syllables without a vowel nucleus.

## 6. Romanization Systems

### Overview of Major Systems

Multiple romanization systems exist for Shanghainese, reflecting different design goals:

| System | Developer | Year | Approach |
|--------|-----------|------|----------|
| **Qian's Pinyin** (钱拼) | Qian Nairong | 2006 | Hanyu Pinyin-based |
| **Wu Chinese Society Pinyin** | Amateur groups | 2005 | IPA-based |
| **Wugniu Pinyin** | Wugniu project | 2016 | IPA-based |
| **Lumazi** | Wu-Chinese.com | 2005 | Hanyu Pinyin-based |
| **Latin Phonetic Method** | Fan Yu | 2001 | French-inspired |

### Handling the Three-Way Contrast

The key challenge for any Wu romanization is representing the three-way laryngeal contrast:

| Contrast | IPA | Qian's Pin | Wu Society | Missionary |
|----------|-----|------------|------------|------------|
| Voiceless unasp. | /p/ | b | p | b |
| Voiceless asp. | /pʰ/ | p | ph | p |
| Voiced | /b/ | bb | b | bh |

**Qian's Pinyin**: Uses Hanyu Pinyin conventions where `b` = /p/ (voiceless unaspirated), `p` = /pʰ/ (aspirated), and `bb` = /b/ (voiced).

**Wu Chinese Society / Wugniu**: Uses IPA-inspired conventions where `p` = /p/, `ph` = /pʰ/, `b` = /b/.

### Tone Marking

| System | Approach |
|--------|----------|
| **Qian's Pin** | Tone numbers (1-5) after syllable |
| **Wu Society** | Tone numbers (1-5) |
| **Lumazi** | Diacritics: acute (high), circumflex (low); marks first syllable only |

**Lumazi rationale**: Since tone sandhi spreads the first syllable's tone, marking only the first syllable is sufficient.

### Sample Transcriptions

"Shanghai" (上海):

| System | Transcription |
|--------|---------------|
| IPA | /zɑ̃.hɛ/ |
| Qian's Pin | zanhei |
| Wu Society | zãhe |
| Lumazi | zânhei |

## 7. Key Contrasts with Other Sinitic Languages

### Wu vs. Mandarin

| Feature | Mandarin | Shanghainese | Implication |
|---------|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Voiced obstruents** | None | /b, d, g, dz, z, v, ʑ, ɦ/ | Critical difference; Wu preserves Middle Chinese voicing |
| **Laryngeal contrast** | 2-way (unasp./asp.) | 3-way (unasp./asp./voiced) | Need Grade 3 or separate series |
| **Retroflex consonants** | /tʂ, tʂʰ, ʂ, ɻ/ | None | Wu lacks retroflexes |
| **Stop codas** | None | /-ʔ/ only | Simpler than Cantonese |
| **Nasal codas** | /-n, -ŋ/ | /-n~ŋ/ (one phoneme) | Wu merged nasal codas |
| **Tones** | 4 | 5 (or 2 + register) | Tone-voicing interaction |
| **Tone sandhi** | Local (T3 sandhi) | Extensive, left-dominant | All non-final syllables change |

### Wu vs. Cantonese

| Feature | Cantonese | Shanghainese | Implication |
|---------|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Voiced obstruents** | None | Full series | Key Wu innovation |
| **Stop codas** | /-p, -t, -k/ | /-ʔ/ (merged) | Cantonese more conservative |
| **Nasal codas** | /-m, -n, -ŋ/ | /-n~ŋ/ | Cantonese more conservative |
| **Tones** | 6 (or 9) | 5 | Similar complexity |
| **Vowel length** | Phonemic | Derived from syllable type | Different systems |
| **Initial ng-** | Yes | Yes | Both have initial velar nasal |

### Wu vs. Southern Min (Hokkien)

| Feature | Hokkien | Shanghainese | Implication |
|---------|---------|--------------|-------------|
| **Voiced obstruents** | /b, g, dz/ | Full series including fricatives | Wu has more voiced consonants |
| **Stop codas** | /-p, -t, -k, -ʔ/ | /-ʔ/ only | Min more conservative |
| **Nasal vowels** | Phonemic | None | Major Min feature absent in Wu |
| **Tones** | 7-8 | 5 | Min more complex |
| **Tone sandhi** | Right-dominant | Left-dominant | Opposite spreading direction |

### The Voiced Obstruent Series: The Critical Difference

The preservation of Middle Chinese voiced obstruents is what fundamentally distinguishes Wu from all other major Chinese language groups:

```
Middle Chinese:  *p   *pʰ   *b    →
Mandarin:        p    pʰ    pʰ/p  (voiced → voiceless, tone split)
Cantonese:       p    pʰ    p     (voiced → unaspirated, tone split)
Hokkien:         p    pʰ    b     (partial preservation)
Shanghainese:    p    pʰ    b     (full preservation + breathy phonation)
```

Wu is unique in preserving the three-way contrast synchronically, with the voiced series realized with distinctive breathy/slack phonation.

## 8. Summary for Tengwar Design

### What Carries Over from Mandarin Mode

1. **Aspiration marking**: Same principle of doubled bow or h-diacritic for /pʰ, tʰ, kʰ/
2. **Alveolo-palatal series**: Similar phonemes /tɕ, tɕʰ, ɕ/ (plus voiced)
3. **Labials /p, m, f/**: Same basic phonemes
4. **Nasal coda concept**: Though Wu has only one merged nasal
5. **Tone marking below**: Same positioning principle

### What Needs New Design

1. **Voiced obstruent series** (CRITICAL):
   - /b, d, g/ — voiced plosives
   - /dʑ/ — voiced affricate
   - /v, z, ʑ, ɦ/ — voiced fricatives
   - Options: Grade 3 tengwar? Separate series? Voicing mark?

2. **Glottal stop coda** /-ʔ/:
   - The only stop coda in Wu
   - Creates checked syllables
   - Options: Tecco? Small órë? Below-mark?

3. **Voiced glottal fricative** /ɦ/:
   - Distinct from /h/
   - No English equivalent (like Dutch g)
   - Needs dedicated representation

4. **Five-tone system with register**:
   - Could use register + contour marks
   - Or extend the four-tone iconographic system
   - Checked tones (T4, T5) may be implicit from /-ʔ/ coda

5. **Left-dominant sandhi**:
   - Only first syllable needs tone mark?
   - Converter must handle tone spreading

### Phoneme Count Summary

| Category | Count | Details |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Initial consonants | 28-30 | Including voiced series |
| Coda consonants | 2 | /-n~ŋ/, /-ʔ/ |
| Vowels (open) | 10 | Long, in unchecked syllables |
| Vowels (checked) | 5 | Short, before /-ʔ/ |
| Syllabic consonants | 4 | /m̩, n̩, ŋ̩, l̩/ |
| Tones | 5 | (or 2 + register) |

### Design Priority

The **three-way laryngeal contrast** is the single most important design challenge. The Tengwar mode must clearly distinguish:
- /p/ (voiceless unaspirated) vs.
- /pʰ/ (voiceless aspirated) vs.
- /b/ (voiced)

...across all relevant consonant series (bilabial stops, alveolar stops, velar stops, alveolo-palatal affricates).
